Abstract. The rapid advancement of Artificial Intelligence (AI) presents unique
opportunities for preserving and revitalizing cultural heritage and traditional
craftsmanship. This paper proposes a novel approach to training AI systems using
archival documentary footage, specifically focusing on ensuring ethical data
practices and empowering Indigenous communities. Our approach is grounded in
the Global Indigenous Data Alliance's “Indigenous Peoples' Rights in Data”
framework, which outlines 12 key principles for ethical data governance.[1] These
principles, including “the rights to possess, consent, refuse, benefit, and use data
originating from a given community,” inform our proposed methodology. By
providing clear guidelines on data governance, we aim to encourage traditionally
closed cultures to share their practices, ensuring that the preservation efforts benefit
the communities themselves. AI systems trained on archival footage and informed
by ethical data practices can be seen as a state-of-the-art form of salvage
ethnography and empower communities to maintain control over their traditional
knowledge and cultural heritage. This approach has the potential to ensure that
diverse artistic practices and traditional craftsmanship continue to thrive in the age
of AI.
Keywords. Artificial Intelligence, craftsmanship, salvage ethnography, robotics
1. Introduction
“Every sensation of every being of the world is a mode through which the world lives
and feels itself,” writes Vinciane Despret. “When a being is no more, the world narrows
all of a sudden, and a part of reality collapses.”[2] This poignant observation underscores
the urgency of our mission to preserve cultural heritage and traditional craftsmanship in
the face of rapid technological change and cultural erosion.
A prime example of a human-AI partnership in cultural preservation is the SkoBot
project, developed by Danielle Boyer, an Ojibwe innovator.[3] This project utilizes AI
models trained on language recordings from community Elders and experts to create
interactive, wearable robots that help preserve indigenous languages and cultural
practices. This approach demonstrates how AI can be a powerful tool for cultural
continuity when developed in collaboration with and for the benefit of indigenous
communities.

Various data sources are crucial in developing diverse and culturally sensitive AI
systems. AI companies are acquiring video footage from content creators to train their
tools.[4] We propose extending this practice to include archival documentary footage,
focusing on materials that document traditional craftsmanship and cultural practices.
Recent developments in AI-powered visual recognition have allowed companies to
dramatically improve searchability within video content libraries.[5] While this allows
for bringing endangered cultural traditions into the AI age with relative technological
ease, we must put guardrails in place to avoid alienating the peoples from whom the
traditions originated.
Data governance is the cornerstone of collaborating with those most at risk—
Indigenous cultures and tradespeople—regarding monetizable skills that robotics
companies will seek to emulate. Some skills—such as sewing—are so broadly practiced
that no group claim them[6], and thus, data governance will do little to protect those
whose livelihoods will be threatened.
Crafts created by specialists without the next generation to take up the tradition are
at risk of disappearance: this runs the gamut from Arizapana, the last in a 500-year
lineage of Incan bridge weavers[7], to crafting parchment and vellum.[8]
While thorough documentation can help researchers recreate lost traditions, such as
Tim Severin’s “The Brendan Voyage,” which depicts him building a leather curragh to
take him across the Atlantic[9], for complex processes, demonstration tends to be better
than documentation because visual illustrations of complex concepts allow for easier
understanding by breaking processes into manageable steps.
We propose an on-premise knowledge base owned by the craftspeople or the culture
from which the craft and any subsequent training materials originated. The knowledge
base will be returned to humanity as a whole upon the retirement of the last trade
practitioner or at a time of the originating culture’s choosing. We seek to eliminate data
harm, “the adverse effects caused by uses of data that damage or set back a person, group,
entity or society’s interests.”[10] For a comprehensive description of data harm, see
Redden, Brand & Terzieva[11].
2. Methodology for AI Training on Archival Footage
2.1. Data Collection
Identify and acquire documentary videos designed to preserve or teach craftsmanship,
such as historical reenactment series. An excellent example of this is the historical
documentary TV series Victorian Farm.[12] In the series, historian Ruth Goodman and
archaeologists Peter Ginn and Alex Langlands move into a Victorian smallholding to
live the life of Victorian farmers, wearing period clothes and only using materials that
would have been available in 1885. The team recreated historical recipes, processes, and
crafts by referencing period sources. Topics covered in the series include stain removal,
threshing wheat, plowing fields, cider-making, pest control, cheese-making, straw
plaiting, and brewing beer.

2.2. Data Processing
Utilize digital tools, such as Adobe Premiere’s Media Intelligence or Linius’ Whizzard
Portal2, to name a few of the many such available resources, to scan and segment videos,
tagging them with relevant metadata for efficient retrieval and analysis.
2.3. Depth Perception Enhancement
Process video clips through Scale-Invariant Monocular Depth Estimation tools to
provide AI systems with crucial depth perception information, enhancing their
understanding of physical processes. “Scale-Invariant Monocular Depth Estimation via
SSI Depth” introduces a novel approach to estimating depth from a single image.[13][14]
This method allows a computer to understand the 3D structure of a scene from just one
2D picture without knowing the actual size of objects in the image. The key innovation
of this approach is its ability to produce accurate depth maps without requiring
information about the scale of objects in the scene.[14] This innovation is beneficial
because it allows the system to work with a wide variety of images, even when the size
of objects is unknown or varies significantly. While depth perception is valuable for
object manipulation[15][16], environmental understanding, transfer learning, and data
augmentation[17], it is not the only factor in training robots from video content.
DeepMind's research demonstrates that other approaches, such as reinforcement learning
and learning from demonstrations, can also be practical in developing dexterous robotic
behaviors.[18] These methods can complement depth perception to create more robust
and capable robotic systems.
2.4. Ethical Data Governance
Implement strict protocols based on the Indigenous Peoples' Rights in Data framework
to ensure community ownership and control over cultural data. The rights outlined by
the Global Indigenous Data Alliance fall under two categories: 1) ‘Data for Governance,’
which primarily relates to the ability of Indigenous communities to access and use data
themselves, and 2) ‘Governance of Data,’ which relates to the ability of Indigenous
communities to steward and externally influence the use of data internally.[19]
3. Potential Application and Benefits
3.1. Ethical Data Governance
AI systems trained on archival footage can help document and recreate traditional
crafting techniques at risk of being lost. Some uncommon crafts may not seem
immediately practical but may be the foundation upon which future cultural ratcheting
can be based. One example is the South Pacific practice of spiderweb fishing, where a
spider web is used as a fishing line. Often, the webs are attached to kites to create a “lure”
that skims across the water's surface.[20]

3.2. Sustainable Production
AI-assisted crafting could promote more sustainable production methods, reducing
reliance on mass-produced goods and their associated environmental impacts. A prime
example of this is pasta; if a robot kitchen can form various pasta shapes from scratch,
then people with access to the robot kitchen will not need to purchase mass-produced
boxes of dried pasta.
3.3. Cultural Education
Interactive AI systems could serve as educational tools, providing immersive
experiences in traditional craftsmanship and cultural practices. An example of this
concept put into practice for a more modern application is the use of humanoid Aura
robots installed in the MSG Sphere in Las Vegas.[21] There is an opportunity to use
humanoid robots as ambassadors of ethnographic salvage for celibate communities like
the Shakers.[22] Only two practicing Shakers are left today, Brother Arnold Hadd and
Sister June Carpenter; the younger of the two (Hadd) is 67.[23]
3.4. Health and Safety
By capturing the expertise of master craftspeople, AI could help mitigate the physical
risks associated with specific crafts, preserving the knowledge while protecting
practitioners from potential health hazards, such as silicosis in relation to
stonemasonry[24], industrial hazards such as carcinogenic exposure to hexavalent
chromium[25], and arthritis.[26]
3.5. Co-Invention
In Melissa A. Schilling’s book Quirky: The Remarkable Story of the Traits, Foibles, and
Genius of Breakthrough Innovators Who Changed the World, she codifies the
characteristics that tend to lead individual people to make extraordinary
breakthroughs.[27] While some of the characteristics are not specifically relevant to a
machine, for example, “opportunities and challenges of an era,” AI mimics essential
attributes of human inventors. Hallucinations in AI are interpreted as unfavorable, but
they can also be analyzed through the lens of a creative breakthrough.[28] The sense of
“separateness” and extreme confidence described by Ms. Schilling are hallmarks of
LLMs. Robots trained on a vast array of artifacts of human culture have a broader corpus
of knowledge from which to draw. The collaboration of innovative humans with a robotic
assistant trained on a broad set of data points yields a connection far more than the sum
of its parts.
4. Potential Unintended Effects
4.1. Individual Autonomy and Withholding
One possible complication is the decision by a “last practitioner” to withhold knowledge,
even when predecessors may have consented to share. This choice should be

acknowledged as a legitimate exercise of individual autonomy and may reflect complex
intergenerational, political, or spiritual concerns. From an ethical standpoint, our
methodology must not override such decisions. Doing so would not only violate the
principle of consent but could also fracture community trust and disrupt nuanced
traditions of knowledge stewardship.
The right to refuse—outlined in the Global Indigenous Data Alliance framework—
is not only collective but personal. AI systems must be designed with mechanisms that
accommodate such refusals, including the possibility that no data may be available or
appropriate for training. This, too, is a form of cultural preservation: one that honors the
sovereignty of silence and restraint.
4.2. Risks of Cultural Essentialism and Fixity
There is also a risk that codifying traditional knowledge into an AI system could
inadvertently fossilize practices that are, by nature, evolving and dynamic.
Craftsmanship often involves improvisation, personal flair, and adaptation over time.
When AI systems learn from archival footage or static documentation, they may reduce
these practices to a fixed “style” or “form,” losing the living dimension of the craft. This
essentialism may be particularly problematic if the AI-generated representations are later
used without community oversight or recontextualization, leading to the creation of
“authenticity standards” that were never meant to exist.
4.3. Data Misuse and Misappropriation
Despite the best efforts to follow ethical data governance principles, the possibility
remains that once encoded, cultural knowledge could be used beyond its intended scope.
For example, generative AI models may synthesize culturally significant artifacts
without appropriate attribution or permissions. These outputs could then be
commercialized or widely disseminated, leading to a new form of digital
misappropriation. To mitigate this, ongoing oversight and enforceable safeguards must
be developed in collaboration with the source communities. Ethical preservation is not a
one-time agreement, but a sustained partnership grounded in trust, reciprocity, and
respect for context.
5. Challenges and Considerations
While this approach offers significant potential for cultural preservation and AI
advancement, it also presents challenges.
5.1. Balancing Tradition and Technology
Efforts must be made to ensure that AI assistance preserves rather than replaces
traditional practices. When advancement rapidly changes processes, replacing manual
labor with faster technology, income sources for the displaced workers may dry up.
Historically, this has contributed to riots and social upheavals. “One implication is that
redistribution matters…policymakers must consider how to support the people who are
most affected. Otherwise, political and social unrest becomes more likely, perhaps

inevitable.”[29] If “The underlying purpose of AI is to allow wealth to access skill while
removing from the skilled the ability to access wealth,”[30] how do we as a society
ensure that skilled workers continue to make a living wage?
5.2. Data Sovereignty
Informed and culturally sensitive adherence to ethical data practices is essential to
earning trust and respect for cultural identity. It includes respecting cultural bearers'
inclinations to withhold secrets of their craft, as exemplified by Chiara Vigo's stance
against monetizing Byssus.[31] One way to address this might be to introduce AI literacy
within Indigenous communities. The introduction of AI literacy programs that enable an
understanding of its capabilities, limitations, and ethical risks, may encourage
community members to recognize the proposed system as a tool of empowerment rather
than a mechanism for digital colonialism.
5.3. Technological Limitations
Current AI systems may struggle to fully capture traditional craftsmanship's nuances and
tacit knowledge. For example, machines cannot properly replicate specific pasta shapes.
Trofie is a short, twisted pasta thick in the middle, with pointed ends. While it has pointed
ends, machine-produced trofie has little difference in the width over the shape of each
piece. Using the pad of one’s palm allows hand-made trofie to be shaped into a geometry
resembling a conch shell.[32] Geometric differences are known to affect everything from
cook times to sauce pairings. Since the pressure that a hand exerts against pasta dough
being pressed into a cutting board is not easy to measure in video form, it is likely that
our proposal will not capture some nuances.
5.4. Ethical Dilemmas
In cases where the last practitioner of a craft decides to invoke their right to refuse to
share knowledge, that decision will inevitably be challenged. We must be prepared to
honor such choices, even if it means losing connection to ancient traditions. For example,
Chiara Vigo is the last person who makes Byssus (sea silk) from the spit of the
endangered Pinna nobilis clam.[31] She is accompanied by the Italian coastguard in
order for her to dive and harvest the thin fibers growing from the tips of the clams. While
she has trained at least one of her grandchildren in the art of how to craft Byssus, her
daughter Maddalena has seemingly chosen a different path in life. Mrs. Vigo’s nine-year-
old granddaughter is being taught how to craft Byssus from raw materials, but training
takes decades.[33] Chiara has repeatedly expressed her complete rejection of writing
down certain parts of the process and profiting off the skill. “...tried to force me to charge
entrance fees and write down my patterns and secrets. But I will defend this sacred oath
with my fingernails as long as I breathe!”[34] Does the Italian coastguard have a moral
right to the knowledge? What happens if the Pinna nobilis clam makes a comeback and
has a thriving population? At what point does the cultural ownership of information end?

6. Conclusion
Our proposed approach offers a promising path to preserving cultural heritage through
ethical AI-human collaboration. By judicious use of archival footage, abiding by strict
principles that govern data, and honoring the rights and wishes of traditional craftspeople,
we can train AI systems to document endangered practices and empower communities
to maintain control over their cultural knowledge. This approach has the potential to
enrich our collective human experience, ensuring that diverse cultural practices and
traditional craftsmanship continue to thrive in the age of AI.
